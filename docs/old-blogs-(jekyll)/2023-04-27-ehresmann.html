    Ehresmann Connection and Gauge Theory

    Ehresmann Connection and Gauge Theory

    Jul 16, 202414 min read

    Ehressmann Connection

    tldr; Connections on Fiber bundles are are specified by vertical bundle-valued one-forms

    One might be familiar with the connection as the Christoffel symbols Γμνρ​(x) or the gauge field Aμ(x). We will review the idea of Ehresmann connection that unifies these 2 seemingly disparate structures in a more abstract framework. It is essential if we are going to generalize the Levi Civita connection (Christoffel symbols) on the tangent bundle to a spin connection on the spin bundle.

    The Ehresmann connection can be defined for any smooth Fiber bundle π:E→M with smooth fiber F=π−1(m), m∈M. If we consider tangent bundle dπ:TE→TM, one can see that dimT(m,f)​TE=dimE=dimM+dimF=dimTm​M+dimF, which implies that dimkerdπ=dimF. Intuitively, the tangent bundle TP has some linear subspace that are “along F”, and this leads to the definition of VE:=ker(dπ:TE→TM) One can look for another subspace that is complementary to VE, i.e. TE=VE⊕HE The intuition is that vectors in HE point in a direction that leads to another fiber, while vectors in VE point in a direction tangent to the current fiber. I would love to add a diagram showing an example with dimM=dimF=1 (the only intuitive diagram one can draw in my opinion).

    In the absence of any additional structure, there are many valid choices of HE, and our choice of HE affects ver(X). This is analogous to saying that the vector space of degree 2 polynomials span(1,x,x2) can be decomposed in many ways

    analogous to TEspan(1,x,x2)span(1,x,x2)​=VE⊕HE=span(1)⊕span(x,x2)⇒ver(x+1)=1=span(1)⊕span(x+1,x2) ⇒ver(x+1)=0​

    Connection Form

    Instead of saying we provide a horizontal subspace at every tangent space of the the tangent bundle TE, it is an equivalent definition to just provide a projection map Φ:TE→VE from which HE=kerΦ can be inferred.

    Punchline: One can say that Φ is a VE-valued one-form on E since it takes a vector in TE and gives an element of VE.

    Ehressmann Connection on G-bundle

    tldr; Connection on G-bundle P is a Lie algebra-valued one-form on TP

    If we consider E to be a principal G-bundle P, then the Φ:TE→VE is a Lie group-valued one-form. Since VE consists of vectors that live in the tangent space T(m,g)​E that have the same dimension as dimG, the vertical subspace V(m,g)​E≅Tg​G≅Te​G≅g is isomorphic to the Lie algebra. So the connection Φ on a G-bundle is specified by a Lie algebra-valued one-form on P (the full bundle P→M).

    However, it turns out that given a local trivialization U×G of P→M, specifying a Lie algebra-valued one-form ωU:TU→g on M actually specifies enough information to specify ΦU​ on π−1(U), which is then glued together with other charts to obtain Φ on the full bundle P. This might seem wild at first since surely projecting Φ onto M to yield ω will lose information with regard to how this one form behaves on the fibers. The answer is that the condition that there is an action of G on the fibers G is constraining enough to reconstruct Φ given ω. These ωU are known as the Yang-Mills fields. There are alot of details that are covered in the excellent lecture 22 by Frederic Schuller.

    Details of Theorem

    The main result is a theorem

    • presented at 21:28 of Schuller Lecture 22 (I follow his notation)
    • Theorem 10.1 in Nakahara
    • Wiki

    Given a section σ:U→P which induces canonical local trivialization h:U×G→P, a g-valued one-form ω on P can be pulled back via σ or h.

    The σ∗ω pullback induces the h∗ω pullback by the following definition h∗ω(m,g)​(v,γ):=Adg−1​((σ∗ω)(v))+(Lg−1​)∗​(γ) where v∈Tm​U, γ∈Tg​G.

    Theorem: This h∗ω satisfies the 2 properties of a connection, namely

    • (h∗ω)(h∗−1​A#)=A
    • (Rg∗​(h∗ω))(h∗−1​X)=Adg−1​(h∗ω(h∗−1​X))

    where A∈g and A#:P→TP is the fundamental vector field on P generated by A, defined pointwise as  Ap∈P#​:=dtd​Rexp(tA)​(p)​t=0​∈Vp​P⊂Tp​P

    Proof of (h∗ω)(h∗−1​A#)=A:

    Since A#∈Vp​P, the pushforward via h yields h∗​A#=(0,γ)∈Tm​U×Tg​G, where γ is γ=dtd​gexp(tA)​t=0​ where (m,g)=h−1(p). It is clear from this that (Lg−1​)∗​γ=A.

    This condition is basically saying that the inverse pullback h−1∗h∗ω=ω satisfies the connection one-form condition ω(A#)=A.

    Proof of (Rg∗​(h∗ω))(h∗−1​X)=Adg−1​(h∗ω(h∗−1​X)):

    To prove this,

    LHS​=(Rk∗​(h∗ω))(h∗−1​X)=h∗ω(Rk∗​h∗−1​X)=(h∗ω)(m,gk)​(v,Rk∗​γ)=Ad(gk)−1​(σ∗ω(v))+L(gk)−1∗Rk∗​γ​=Adk−1​Adg−1​(σ∗ω(v))+Lk−1∗​Rk∗​Lg−1∗​γ=Adk−1​(Adg−1​(σ∗ω(v))+Lg−1∗​γ)=Adk−1​(h∗ω(h∗−1​X))=RHS​

    So the above calculations has shown that given a g-valued one-form ωU on U and a section σ:U→π−1(U), there exists a unique connection one-form ω on π−1(U) such that ωU=σ∗ω. The constructed connection depends on σ.

    ⇐ construction: Split P into charts Ui. Then each local trivialization induces a section σi​:M→P. The pullback of ω on P via σi​ will give an ωUi​ on Ui​, which is a Yang-mills field ωM on the base manifold M after gluing. The gluing is what we call “gauge transformations of A” or “how Γ transforms under change in coordinates”.

    ⇒ construction: Split P into charts Ui. On each chart, the one-form ωUi on Ui induces a one-form (which is actually h∗ω) on the local trivialisation h of π−1(Ui) via the theorem above. These h∗ω can be glued together to construct a one-form ω on P.

    Clearing the Ambiguity in Nakahara’s Notation

    I learned alot from Nakahara’s book, but I find their section on connection one-form slightly ambiguous. For the connection one-form on P, they write ωi​≡gi−1​π∗Ai​gi​+gi−1​dgi​ where Ai​ is the Yang Mills one-form on the base manifold M. The first term gi−1​π∗Ai​gi​ actually means Adgi−1​​(π∗Ai​(X)) where Adgi−1​​:Te​G→Te​G is the adjoint action of G on g, and X∈Tp​P. The Adg−1​(Y) and g−1Yg, Y∈g notations coincide for matrix Lie groups, where the multiplication between g and Y is matrix multiplication. I still prefer to talk purely in non-matrix notation because I find it’s clearer.

    Because X=H+V and V∈kerπ∗, the above actually reduces to

    Adgi−1​​(Ai​(v))

    where v∈Tm​Ui≅Hp​P is a horizontal vector. The first term takes care of the vertical component of X.

    The second term is extremely ambiguous. gi−1​ is simply just Lg−1∗​. Trouble comes from dgi​. Usually one uses dg to denote the pushforward of g. But in this case, dg actually means γ∈Vp​P≅Tg​G. If I include the implicit annotations, ωi​(m,g)​(X)≡Adgi−1​​(Ai​(hor(X)))+Lg−1∗​(ver(X)) So how does one justify dg=γ? g is interpreted as a map R→G in the local trivialization h−1(p)=(m,g). A vector is the derivative of a curve at one point of the curve, so given a curve C in P which leads to vector X=dtdC​, we actually mean to define dg≡dtd​g(C(t))∈Vp​P i.e. derivative of the G fiber “coordinate” of our curve C(t)=(m(t),g(t)). dg:TM→VP is the pushforward of the map g:M→P. So dg(π∗X)=ver(X).

    tldr; dg is ver(X).

    Gluing of Connection One-Form

    Given 2 overlapping local trivializations Ui∩Uj (which induces 2 local sections σi​,σj​:Ui∩Uj→P), we would like to find how σi∗​ω is related to σj∗​ω. The answer is obtained by investigating how they act on a vector X∈Tm​(Ui∩Uj). We need to calculate σi∗​X∈Tσi​(m)​P in terms of σj∗​X∈Tσj​(m)​P. Or in terms of the local trivialisation (hi​∘σi​)∗​X∈T(m,ei​)​Ui×G and (hj​∘σj​)∗​X∈T(m,ej​)​Uj×G.

    We know that tij​:Ui∩Uj→G is the transition map defined as the unique (guaranteed since G acts freely and transitively on the fibers of P) element tij​(m)∈G such that Rtij​(m)​(p)=hj​∘hi−1​(p) This definition can be rephrased in a more friendly manner as

    σj​(m)=σi​(m)∙R​tij​(m)

Right G-invariance of the horizontal bundle Rtij​∗​:Hσi​(m)​P→Hσj​(m)​P means horizontal gets mapped onto horizontal.     Calculating Christoffel Symbols

    Christoffel symbols Γ is just the connection one-form ω:Tp​M→g on the base manifold of a tangent frame bundle (which can be shown to be a GL(d)-principal bundle). The goal will be to calculate how Γ changes when we change coordinates.

    The nice thing is that a coordinate chart x:U(1)→Rd on U(1)⊂M already defines a local trivialization of the GL(d)-bundle. Namely, the d coordinate functions xi:U(1)→R can be used to define a section σ(1):U(1)→F(Tσ(1)(x)​M)

    σ(1)(x)=(∂x1∂​,∂x2∂​,... ,∂xd∂​)

    A vector X∈Tp​M can be written as X=Xμ∂μ​, with μ=1,... ,dimM. Hence the “Yang-Mills” one-form on the base manifold M takes the form σ(1)ω:=Γμ​dxμ, where Γμ​∈g.

    Since G=GL(d), the Lie algebra is just g=gl(d), consisting of all d×d real matrices. So Γμ​=Γijμ​ where i,j=1,... ,d are indices for the matrix Lie algebra gl(d).

    We are interested in the transformations laws for this Γ. Suppose we have another chart y:U(2)M→Rd. Then on the intersection U(1)∩U(2), if γ(t)∈M is a curve with tangent vector X=γ˙​(0),

    σj∗​ω(X)Γμ(2)​dyμ(X)​=Adtij−1​​(σi∗​ω(X))+Ltij−1​∗​tij∗​X=Ω−1(Γμ(1)​dxμ(X))Ω+Ω−1dtd​Ω(γ(t))​t=0​​

    The last term can be rewritten as

    Ω−1∂xμ∂Ω(x)​dtdxμ(γ(t))​=Ω−1∂xμ∂Ω(x)​Xμ

    So with all the indices flashed out,

    Γ(2)ijμ​dyμ(X)​=(Ω−1)ik​(Γ(1)klμ​dxμ(X))Ωlj​+(Ω−1)ik​∂μ​Ωkj​Xμ​

    The vector X=Xμ∂xμ∂​ expressed in the 2nd chart is X=X~μ∂yμ∂​, with Xμ=∂yν∂xμ​X~ν So

    dxμ(X)=Xμ=∂yν∂xμ​X~ν

    Putting this back into the above expression,

    {\Gamma^{(2) i}}_{j\mu} \tilde X^\mu &= {(\Omega^{-1})^i}_{k} \left( {\Gamma^{(1)k}}_{l\mu} \frac{\partial x^\mu}{\partial y^\nu}\tilde X^\nu \right) {\Omega^l}_j + {(\Omega^{-1})^i}_k\partial_\mu {\Omega^k}_j X^\mu \end{aligned}$$ Now the transition functions $\Omega:M\rightarrow GL(d)$, or in coordinates, ${\Omega^i}_j: M \rightarrow \mathbb R$ are actually $${\Omega^i}_j = \frac{\partial x^i}{\partial y^j}$$ $${(\Omega^{-1})^i}_j = \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^j}$$ so $$\begin{aligned} {\Gamma^{(2) i}}_{j\mu} \tilde X^\mu &= \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^k} \left( {\Gamma^{(1)k}}_{l\mu} \frac{\partial x^\mu}{\partial y^\nu}\tilde X^\nu \right) \frac{\partial x^l}{\partial y^j} + \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial^2 x^k}{\partial y^\mu \partial y^j} \tilde X^\mu \end{aligned}$$ Removing the $X$ (more rigorously, choosing $X=\delta^{\mu\rho} \frac{\partial}{\partial y^\rho}$), $$\begin{aligned} {\Gamma^{(2) i}}_{j\rho} &= {\Gamma^{(1)k}}_{l\mu} \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial x^\mu}{\partial y^\rho} \frac{\partial x^l}{\partial y^j} + \frac{\partial y^i}{\partial x^k} \frac{\partial^2 x^k}{\partial y^\rho \partial y^j} \end{aligned}$$ Not all indices of $\Gamma$ are the same type! 1 of them is spacetime, the other 2 actually label the Lie algebra. ### Memorisation Trick When we learn this in GR we usually get giddy just trying to write it with the correct indices.
